## P3 — "Why You Like The Regulars Before You've Even Talked" (science fact — new citation)
**Status:** draft
**Format:** image · Instagram (`platform: instagram`)
**Pillar:** science fact (capped at 1 combined science/quote slot this batch, per contract v3
rule 4)
**Photo:** `pexels-19146676-group-track-run.jpg` — a diverse group jogging together on a track.

**Science fact + source (verified 2026-07-14 via WebSearch):** Robert Zajonc, "Attitudinal
Effects of Mere Exposure," *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 1968 — the "mere-
exposure effect." Repeated exposure to a stimulus (including unfamiliar faces) reliably increases
how positively people rate it, even with no interaction involved. Replicated in 200+ studies since
(Bornstein's 1989 meta-analysis; a later meta-analysis of 208 experiments found the effect robust,
r=0.26). Not a repeat of Blue Zones (longevity), rowers'-high (pain tolerance), loneliness
(Holt-Lunstad), synchrony/trust (Wiltermuth-Heath), or the NBA touch study (Kraus/Huang/Keltner),
all already used in batches 06–13.

**Poster spec:** KeepItUp mark top-left. Eyebrow (neon-green): "THE SCIENCE OF SHOWING UP."
Headline block (off-white on anthracite scrim): "SEE THE SAME PEOPLE AT THE SAME SATURDAY GAME A
FEW WEEKS RUNNING, AND YOU START LIKING THEM MORE — BEFORE YOU'VE EVEN TALKED." Citation subtext:
"Zajonc, Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 1968." Footer band: wordmark + "open
worldwide, early access." Planned composite: `b14p3-why-you-like-the-regulars.png`.

**Caption:** "In 1968, psychologist Robert Zajonc ran a simple experiment: show people the same
unfamiliar image a few times, and they start rating it more positively — no interaction needed,
just repeated exposure. It's called the mere-exposure effect, and it's one of the most-replicated
findings in psychology, confirmed again and again over 200+ follow-up studies since (Zajonc,
'Attitudinal Effects of Mere Exposure,' 1968).

It works on people too. Show up to the same small game a few weekends running, and the regulars
start feeling familiar — friendly, even — before you've had a real conversation. KeepItUp is a
free app to organize or join a small local game near you, the kind you can actually keep showing
up to. Open worldwide — early access, link in bio."

**CTA:** "Open worldwide — early access, link in bio."

**Concept + source (verified 2026-07-14 via WebSearch):** sociologist Ray Oldenburg coined "third
place" in *The Great Good Place* (1989) — neutral, unstructured, inexpensive places that are
neither home nor work, where people gather and community happens (cafes, parks, local clubs,
gyms). Oldenburg's core argument: third places declined sharply in the decades after WWII, and
that decline tracks with rising isolation. This is a well-established sociological framework
(Wikipedia, UNESCO Courier, Project for Public Spaces all confirm the concept and its author/date)
— presented as a framework, not a scientific study, and not overclaimed as such.
