# Content batch 08 — why KeepItUp: two real pains, plain answers

**Author:** social-media-growth strategist · **Created:** 2026-07-05 · **Status:** all `draft`

**Owner directive (2026-07-05):** stop assuming people already get why KeepItUp is different —
spell it out against two pains the owner named directly:

1. **The group chat where the game never happens.** People try to organize games in a group
   chat and it turns into unread messages, dead polls, no venue, no game.
2. **Paying to stand around at a singles event.** People pay for tickets to mingle events and
   stand around with a drink, forced small talk, awkward vibes.

Every post below answers "why KeepItUp" against one of these two pains, in plain English, image-led
with a real photo via `body.assets`. No competitor or specific event company is named — the pain
is described generically (a "typical singles event," "the group chat"), except **WhatsApp**, which
the owner cleared to name neutrally and kindly as the place group plans quietly die (not a knock on
WhatsApp itself). Only implemented features are referenced: small local meetups, request-to-join,
host accepts, approximate location privacy, group chat limited to people actually confirmed, the
dating/friendship/group intent field, and that KeepItUp is free. All copy stays **worldwide /
early-access** — no launch city, no invented traction.

## P5 — IG image (deep) · Why doing something together beats talking about it
**Status:** draft
**Pain:** both pains, from a different angle — talking (chat polls, mixer small talk) doesn't
build connection the way doing something together does.
**Science fact + source:** Aron, A., Norman, C.C., Aron, E.N., McKenna, C., & Heyman, R.E.
(2000), "Couples' Shared Participation in Novel and Arousing Activities and Experienced
Relationship Quality," *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 78(2), 273–284. In lab
experiments, pairs who did something new and a little exciting together — not just talked or did
something routine — reported feeling measurably closer afterward. (Framed honestly: the original
study is about established couples; we're generalizing the underlying mechanism, not claiming it
was tested on strangers.)
**Pillar:** Pain-led + science fact · **Hypothesis:** H-PAIN-doingvstalking (a sourced,
honestly-framed "doing beats talking" fact, tied to both pains, wins saves as well as our pure
science-fact posts)
**Photo:** `/brand/social/photos/pexels-5319325-jog-bridge.jpg` (people jogging together,
contemplative)
**Treatment:** blend — a small label top-left on a small dark scrim ("WHY DOING BEATS TALKING"),
photo otherwise clean; full explanation lives in the caption (overlay to be baked onto the base
photo above).

**Caption:** "There's a well-known study from 2000 by researcher Arthur Aron and colleagues. They
had couples do something new and a bit exciting together, or something routine, and measured how
close they felt afterward. The pairs who did something new together — not just talked — felt
closer.

We think the same logic holds before people even know each other well. A profile and a few
messages only tell you so much. Ten minutes on a football pitch or a group run tells you how
someone actually shows up — do they laugh things off, do they check if you're okay, do they
actually enjoy themselves.

Talking is a fine start. Doing something together is what tells you if you want to keep talking.

KeepItUp is open worldwide — early access, link in bio."
